01Executive Summary

Chicago made its choice early: the Bulls spent their cap space in the first 24 hours of free agency — Norman Powell (2yr/$45M) for real scoring, Zach Collins for the frontcourt, and a re-signed Josh Giddey ($25M/yr) to run it — around a young core of Matas Buzelis, No. 4 pick Caleb Wilson, Rob Dillingham and No. 15 pick Dailyn Swain. What's left is a $9.4M room exception, one roster spot, and the franchise-defining question this guide is built to answer: is this a playoff push or a bridge year for the kids?

The Headline

Powell (21.7 ppg last season) is the best pure scorer the Bulls have added in years, and Giddey–Powell–Buzelis–Wilson is a real offensive foundation. The books stay clean: well under the tax, no apron anxiety, every tool available.

The Constraint

Not money — identity. The roster leans heavily offense (Giddey, Powell, Dillingham, Vučević-less frontcourt) with a bottom-third projected defense. The $9.4M room exception and the Patrick Williams/Okoro salaries are the tools to fix it.

The Wildcard

Caleb Wilson's readiness. If the No. 4 pick defends at an NBA level immediately, the timeline questions answer themselves — the Bulls can chase a top-6 East seed in a conference where only three teams are clearly better.

Front-office verdict

The Powell signing says "win now"; the Wilson pick says "2028". Both can be true in a softened East — but only if the summer's remaining moves buy defense: a point-of-attack wing with the room exception and a defensive consolidation trade from the Williams/Okoro/Smith salary pile. Grade for the offseason to date: B — real talent added, direction still unpriced.

Three priorities from today to opening night: (1) Spend the $9.4M room exception on the best available defensive wing/guard. (2) Consolidate two mid-salaries into one two-way starter (the McDaniels track). (3) Set a hard December checkpoint: top-8 East → buy at the deadline; below it → flip Powell for picks.

02League Overview

The 2026-27 salary environment is the tightest in NBA history. The second apron's punitive machinery — frozen draft picks, no aggregation, no cash in trades, a hard cap — has pushed nearly every contender toward youth and internal development. Cap space is scarce league-wide — the RFA class resolved fast and cheap (Giddey re-signed, Grimes took Lakers money, Kuminga boomeranged back to Atlanta), with Cam Thomas and Jalen Duren the last standoffs.

What the flexibility means

Having used cap room, Chicago's MLE/BAE are gone — but the toolbox is still strong:

  • $9.4M room exception — a real weapon for a defensive wing; most rivals can only offer minimums.
  • Massive tax cushion (~$47M) — can absorb salary in trades that taxed teams must dump.
  • Aggregation fully legal: Williams ($18M) + Okoro ($11.8M) legally returns up to ~$60M.

The apron ladder — what each line actually does

Luxury Tax · $200.43M

A dollar penalty, not a hard restriction. Teams pay an escalating tax on every dollar over the line ($1.50 → $1.75 → $2.50 → $3.25 …). Repeater teams (taxed in 3 of the last 4 years) pay a punitive surcharge on top. You can still sign, trade, and use every exception.

First Apron · $209.02M

The first real handcuff. A team over it cannot: use the full non-taxpayer MLE (drops to the ~$5.7M taxpayer MLE), use the Bi-Annual Exception, acquire players via sign-and-trade, sign a buyout player who earned above the NT-MLE, or take back more salary than it sends in a trade. Using the NT-MLE, BAE, or a sign-and-trade hard-caps you here for the whole season.

Second Apron · $221.69M

The nuclear tier. Everything above plus: no MLE at all, cannot aggregate two+ salaries in a trade, cannot send cash in trades, cannot trade a first-round pick seven years out (and it gets frozen/moved to the back of round one). This is designed to force even the richest owners to break up rosters.
