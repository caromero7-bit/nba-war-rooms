01Executive Summary

San Antonio's 2025-26 season ended one series short: the Spurs won the West — beating 62-win Oklahoma City 111–103 in a Game 7 for the franchise's first Finals since 2014 — and then ran out of halfcourt offense against New York, losing the Finals in five. The diagnosis is unusually clean for a Finals team: the roster is young, deep, defensively elite, and one proven shot-creator short. This guide is the plan to buy that last piece without breaking the Wembanyama timeline.

The Headline

Victor Wembanyama is the best defensive player alive and the franchise's everything; he becomes eligible for a projected 5yr/$251M rookie-max extension that would start in 2027-28. De'Aaron Fox ($49.5M) gives the Spurs a downhill engine; Castle, Harper and Bryant give them the league's best young guard depth.

The Work So Far

Early moves target the Finals gap at the margins: Tobias Harris (2yr/$31M) adds veteran forward shot-making, Julian Champagnie re-signed (3yr/$45M), and the draft added Jayden Quaintance (No. 20), Tarris Reed Jr. (No. 26), Ja'Kobi Gillespie (No. 42) and Maliq Brown (No. 44).

The Wildcard

LeBron James. San Antonio is the one suitor that can engineer real money for him rather than a minimum — and he is precisely the Finals-level creator and closer the Knicks series exposed the need for. The suitor ranking has GSW ahead, but the Spurs' pitch is the strongest basketball case.

Front-office verdict

Unlike almost every other contender, San Antonio is not hard-capped and not apron-squeezed — roughly $188M committed (projected) against a $200.43M tax line, with the full mid-level available and movable mid-size salaries (Keldon Johnson $17.5M expiring, Luke Kornet ~$10.3M) as clean trade ballast. The asset chest (own picks plus a strong swap/second stock) remains among the league's best. Grade for the offseason to date: A- — smart marginal adds, with the big swing still to come.

Three priorities from today to opening night: (1) Land a Finals-level creator — LeBron with engineered room, or Simons/Markkanen by trade. (2) Keep the defensive identity intact — don't overpay for offense with Wemby's supporting shell. (3) Re-sign the useful vets (Olynyk, Barnes) on minimums and preserve July flexibility for the swing.

02League Overview

The 2026-27 salary environment is the tightest in NBA history. The second apron's punitive machinery — frozen draft picks, no aggregation, no cash in trades, a hard cap — has pushed nearly every contender toward youth and internal development. Cap space is scarce league-wide — the RFA class resolved fast and cheap (Giddey re-signed, Grimes took Lakers money, Kuminga boomeranged back to Atlanta), with Cam Thomas and Jalen Duren the last standoffs.

The apron ladder — what each line actually does

Luxury Tax · $200.43M

A dollar penalty, not a hard restriction. Teams pay an escalating tax on every dollar over the line ($1.50 → $1.75 → $2.50 → $3.25 …). Repeater teams (taxed in 3 of the last 4 years) pay a punitive surcharge on top. You can still sign, trade, and use every exception.

First Apron · $209.02M

The first real handcuff. A team over it cannot: use the full non-taxpayer MLE (drops to the ~$5.7M taxpayer MLE), use the Bi-Annual Exception, acquire players via sign-and-trade, sign a buyout player who earned above the NT-MLE, or take back more salary than it sends in a trade. Using the NT-MLE, BAE, or a sign-and-trade hard-caps you here for the whole season.

Second Apron · $221.69M

The nuclear tier. Everything above plus: no MLE at all, cannot aggregate two+ salaries in a trade, cannot send cash in trades, cannot trade a first-round pick seven years out (and it gets frozen/moved to the back of round one). This is designed to force even the richest owners to break up rosters.
