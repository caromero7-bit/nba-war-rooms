01Executive Summary

The 2026 offseason will be remembered as the summer a generational talent changed conferences. On June 24, the Milwaukee Bucks traded Giannis Antetokounmpo and Bobby Portis to the Miami Heat for Tyler Herro, Kel'el Ware, Jaime Jaquez Jr., Kašparas Jakučionis, the No. 13 pick (Nate Ament), unprotected 2031 and 2033 first-round picks, a 2030 pick swap, and a 2033 second. In one stroke, Miami vaulted from play-in mediocrity into the East's title conversation — and hard-capped itself at the first apron in the process.

The Headline

Giannis + Bam is the best defensive frontcourt in basketball. Miami's ceiling is a top-3 defense and a top-4 East seed. The floor risk is spacing: the Heat traded away Herro's shot-creation and let All-Star Norman Powell walk to Chicago (2yr/$45M) because the apron left no money to keep him.

The Constraint

Hard-capped at $209.02M. Miami opened with ~$20.5M of first-apron space; after signing Hardaway Jr. and Fontecchio it has roughly $10.7M left for 2–3 spots. Every dollar matters. The last upgrade runs through the partial MLE, a Jović/Wiggins-based trade, or a minimum-contract sniper.

The Wildcard

LeBron James left the Lakers and is an unrestricted free agent who cannot sign until July 6. A Heat run at LeBron is a long shot under the apron — but the decision matrix shows exactly what it would take.

Front-office verdict

Miami went all-in on a two-year championship window (Giannis is 31; his contract runs through 2027-28 with a 2027-28 player option). The organization's job now is not to add star power — it is to surround two elite bigs with shooting, a lead ball-handler, and playable wing depth at minimum-to-MLE prices. This guide's Miami sections lay out that plan in full. Grade for the offseason to date: B+ — an A for ambition and asset-efficiency on the star, docked for the spacing bill now coming due.

Three priorities from today to opening night: (1) Land a movement shooter for the MLE or minimum. (2) Solidify the lead-guard spot behind Davion Mitchell. (3) Keep a trade exception and Jović/Wiggins salary live as deadline ammunition for a mid-size upgrade.

02League Overview

The 2026-27 salary environment is the tightest in NBA history. The second apron's punitive machinery — frozen draft picks, no aggregation, no cash in trades, a hard cap — has pushed nearly every contender toward youth and internal development. Cap space is scarce league-wide — the RFA class resolved fast and cheap (Giddey re-signed, Grimes took Lakers money, Kuminga boomeranged back to Atlanta), with Cam Thomas and Jalen Duren the last standoffs.

What the hard cap means

By using the 125% expanded trade exception on Giannis, Miami is hard-capped at the first apron ($209.02M) — they cannot exceed it for any reason all year. Practical effects:

  • They can use the Non-Taxpayer MLE (~$14.1M) — but only if it keeps them under $209.02M. In practice they can offer a partial MLE.
  • They cannot take back more salary than they send in trades.
  • Every minimum deal (~$2.3M) eats into the $18.1M cushion.
  • Realistic in-season spending: ~$18.1M total across 4-5 signings.

The apron ladder — what each line actually does

Luxury Tax · $200.43M

A dollar penalty, not a hard restriction. Teams pay an escalating tax on every dollar over the line ($1.50 → $1.75 → $2.50 → $3.25 …). Repeater teams (taxed in 3 of the last 4 years) pay a punitive surcharge on top. You can still sign, trade, and use every exception.

First Apron · $209.02M

The first real handcuff. A team over it cannot: use the full non-taxpayer MLE (drops to the ~$5.7M taxpayer MLE), use the Bi-Annual Exception, acquire players via sign-and-trade, sign a buyout player who earned above the NT-MLE, or take back more salary than it sends in a trade. Using the NT-MLE, BAE, or a sign-and-trade hard-caps you here for the whole season.

Second Apron · $221.69M

The nuclear tier. Everything above plus: no MLE at all, cannot aggregate two+ salaries in a trade, cannot send cash in trades, cannot trade a first-round pick seven years out (and it gets frozen/moved to the back of round one). This is designed to force even the richest owners to break up rosters.
